Research Overview
The goal of my research is to develop accurate, efficient and fair evaluation systems. I draw inspirations from psychology to model real-world phenomena, develop theoretical guarantees using tools from statistics and computer science, conduct crowdsourcing experiments, and implement policy changes that real-world impacts. Specifically, I identify, analyze and improve three key components: algorithms, people, and designs.
  • Algorithms: It's important to think holistically about the objectives and constraints, which describe intricate connections and tradeoffs underlying evaluation systems. I develop algorithms that effectively aggregate evaluation data, integrating accuracy with the following considerations:

    • Fairness: We consider a crowdsourcing model (Bradley-Terry model) that infers item quality from pairwise comparisons. We propose a simple modification to the widely-used maximum likelihood estimator, which is simple to implement and enjoys a win-win in the bias-accuracy tradeoff – that is, it significantly improves fairness while achieving optimal accuracy. [paper | talk]

    • Uncertainty: In applications such as hiring and admissions, part of the goal is to successfully recruit a pre-defined number of candidates. We formulate a Knapsack-style combinatorial optimization problem, and present theoretical guarantees for various algorithms that simultaneously consider quality and uncertainty.

  • People: Algorithms do not operate in isolation; it is crucial to consider where input data come from – human evaluators. People are not perfectly rational agents, and they have biases even if they consciously try to avoid them. My work identifies three sources of human biases:

    • Miscalibration: People have different numerical scales when reporting ratings. We design algorithms that take into account the miscalibration of people – for example, some people tend to be lenient and give higher scores, and some others tend to be strict. Our algorithms are provably more robust to people’s miscalibration, and hence make the evaluation more fair. [paper]

    • Personal experience: Evaluators are influenced by their experiences. For example, in teaching evaluation, students often give higher ratings if they receive higher grades. We capture such tendencies by a natural monotonic constraint, and propose an estimator that reduces the bias in a data-dependent fashion, without prior knowledge on the extent of the bias. [paper | talk]

    • Ordering effect: Evaluations are made sequentially in applications such as sports/arts competitions and hiring. We provide a model to capture the nature of such sequential evaluation, supported by crowdsourcing experiments. We propose a ranking estimator that is both efficient and optimal (in certain notions), whereas using the ranking naively induced by the scores is provably not optimal. [paper]

  • Designs: People engage with algorithms, and algorithms influence human behaviors. I study design and policy decisions that underpin the interactions between people and algorithms in evaluation systems. In large-scale evaluation tasks, we examine different allocation schemes that distribute the task to reviewers, and provide a comprehensive comparison between them using a mixture of theoretical and experimental approaches. [paper]
Beyond technical work, I desire to extend my research to real policy improvements in practice. For example, we compile the data and evaluate the gender distribution in award-winning papers in 16 top computer science conferences in the past 10 years, which shows prominent differences across conferences. [blog post] We also consider the biases caused by the alphabetical ordering practice in scientific publication. Taking cognizance of the biases arising from alphabetical ordering, the Machine Learning Department at CMU has randomized the ordering of students and faculty on its webpages, along with many other institutions. [blog post]
